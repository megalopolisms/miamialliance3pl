What Is Dropshipping? A Clear Definition

Dropshipping is a fulfillment method where you never purchase, store, or handle the product you sell. When a customer places an order on your online store, that order is forwarded to a third-party supplier — usually a manufacturer or wholesale distributor — who then ships the product directly to your customer. You act as the storefront and marketing engine. The supplier acts as the warehouse and shipping department. Your profit is the difference between the retail price you charge and the wholesale cost plus shipping the supplier charges you.

Here is how a typical dropshipping transaction works in practice:

  • Customer orders a product on your Shopify store for $39.99.
  • Your store automatically sends the order to your supplier. This happens through a dropshipping app like DSers, Spocket, or a direct API connection to the supplier's system.
  • The supplier picks, packs, and ships the product directly to the customer. The package may come from a warehouse in China, the United States, or wherever the supplier operates.
  • The supplier charges you their wholesale price plus shipping. For this example, that might be $18.00 for the product and $4.00 for ePacket or standard shipping — a total cost of $22.00.
  • Your gross profit on this order is $17.99 ($39.99 minus $22.00) before marketing costs, platform fees, and payment processing.

The appeal is obvious: zero inventory investment. You do not need a warehouse, you do not need to negotiate freight shipments from manufacturers, and you do not need $10,000 to $50,000 in starting capital to buy your first production run. You can launch a store with nothing more than a Shopify subscription, a supplier relationship, and a marketing budget. That low barrier to entry is why dropshipping exploded in popularity over the past decade and why it remains one of the most common entry points for first-time e-commerce entrepreneurs.

But the model has structural limitations that become impossible to ignore as your store grows — limitations in margins, shipping speed, quality control, and branding that we will dissect in this guide.

What Is 3PL Fulfillment? A Clear Definition

Third-party logistics (3PL) fulfillment is a model where you own your inventory but outsource the storage, picking, packing, and shipping of orders to a professional warehouse. You purchase products from a manufacturer in bulk — typically at significant wholesale discounts — and ship that inventory to a 3PL warehouse. The 3PL stores your products on pallets or shelves in their facility. When a customer places an order on your online store, the 3PL's warehouse management system (WMS) receives the order automatically, and the warehouse team picks the items, packs them according to your specifications (including branded packaging, inserts, and custom materials), generates a carrier shipping label, and ships the package directly to your customer.

Here is how a typical 3PL-fulfilled transaction works:

  • You purchase 1,000 units of your product from a manufacturer at $8.00 per unit. Total inventory investment: $8,000. You ship the inventory to your 3PL warehouse.
  • The 3PL receives and stores your inventory. Storage cost: $25 per pallet per month. If your 1,000 units fit on 3 pallets, that is $75/month in storage.
  • A customer orders the product on your store for $39.99.
  • The 3PL picks, packs, and ships the order. Pick and pack fee: $2.50. Shipping via bulk-negotiated carrier rate: $5.50 (versus $8.00+ at retail rates). Total fulfillment cost per order: $8.00.
  • Your cost per unit sold: $8.00 (product) + $8.00 (fulfillment) = $16.00. Your gross profit: $23.99 per order — compared to $17.99 in the dropshipping example above.

The tradeoff is clear: 3PL fulfillment requires upfront capital to purchase inventory, but it delivers higher margins, faster shipping, complete brand control, and a customer experience that builds loyalty and repeat purchases. You are not selling someone else's product from someone else's warehouse with someone else's shipping timeline. You are selling your product, from your warehouse partner, with your branding, delivered in 2-3 days instead of 7-21 days.

When Dropshipping Makes Sense

Despite the margin and speed disadvantages, dropshipping is a legitimate fulfillment model in specific circumstances. Here is when it is the right choice:

  • You are testing a new market or product category. Before committing $5,000 to $20,000 in inventory, dropshipping lets you validate demand with zero financial risk. List products, run ads, see what sells, and only then invest in inventory for winners.
  • You have very limited startup capital. If your total budget is $1,000, you cannot afford to purchase inventory and pay a 3PL. Dropshipping lets you start selling immediately and reinvest profits until you have enough capital to transition to owned inventory.
  • You sell products with extreme variety or long-tail SKU counts. A store with 500+ SKUs across dozens of categories cannot realistically warehouse every item. Dropshipping the slow-moving tail while warehousing your top sellers is an efficient allocation of capital.
  • You sell oversized, heavy, or fragile items with high storage costs. If your product is a 200-pound piece of furniture that costs $50 per month to store on a pallet, dropshipping directly from the manufacturer might make better financial sense — especially if order volume is low.
  • You are using it as a discovery tool, not a permanent business model. The smartest entrepreneurs use dropshipping as market research: test products, identify winners, and then transition winners to owned inventory with a 3PL. Dropshipping is the scouting phase; 3PL is the buildout phase.
💡
Key Insight: Dropshipping is best understood as a testing mechanism, not a scaling mechanism. It lets you discover what sells without risk. But once a product is proven — consistent orders, validated demand, repeat customers — the economically rational move is to transition that product to bulk purchasing and 3PL fulfillment. Every month you continue dropshipping a proven winner is a month of unnecessarily low margins.

When 3PL Fulfillment Makes Sense

3PL fulfillment becomes the clearly superior model once certain conditions are met. Here is when it makes sense to transition:

  • You are doing 50+ orders per month consistently. At this volume, the per-order economics of 3PL fulfillment start to pull ahead of dropshipping. You have enough data to forecast demand, order the right quantities from your manufacturer, and justify the monthly storage costs.
  • You want to build a real brand, not just a storefront. Brands are built on consistent customer experiences — branded packaging, fast shipping, reliable delivery windows, professional unboxing moments. None of this is possible with dropshipping. A 3PL fulfills orders with your branded boxes, custom tissue paper, thank-you inserts, and promotional materials. Your customer receives a package that looks and feels like it came from a real company, not a generic poly mailer from a random warehouse.
  • You need fast, consistent shipping speeds. A 3PL warehouse in Miami can ship to the entire Southeast in 1-2 days and 80% of the continental U.S. in 2-3 days by ground. That is the difference between a 4.5-star review ("fast shipping, great packaging") and a 2-star review ("took three weeks to arrive, came in a plain bag").
  • You sell on multiple channels. If you are on Shopify, Amazon, WooCommerce, Etsy, and wholesale simultaneously, you need a single inventory pool managed by a professional 3PL that integrates with all of those platforms. Dropshipping across multiple channels with different suppliers is a logistics nightmare that guarantees inventory discrepancies and overselling.
  • Your return rate is above 5%. Returns are a reality of e-commerce, especially in fashion and accessories. A 3PL handles returns processing — receiving the returned item, inspecting it, restocking sellable units, and updating your inventory counts — without you having to deal with the physical logistics.
  • You are spending too much time on fulfillment and not enough on growth. If you are packing orders yourself and it is eating into your marketing, product development, and strategic time, a 3PL frees you from the operational grind so you can focus on revenue-generating activities.

The Hybrid Model: Combining Dropshipping and 3PL Fulfillment

The most sophisticated e-commerce operators do not choose between dropshipping and 3PL — they combine both strategically. The hybrid model works like this:

1

Warehouse Your Top Sellers at a 3PL

Identify your top 10-20 SKUs — the products that generate 80% of your revenue. These are your proven winners with predictable demand. Purchase these in bulk from your manufacturer at the best wholesale price and store them at your 3PL warehouse. Every order for these products ships same-day in branded packaging with 2-3 day delivery. These are your margin drivers, your brand builders, and your customer loyalty engines.

2

Dropship Your Extended Catalog

For your remaining 50-200 SKUs — the slow movers, seasonal items, and products you are still testing — use dropshipping. These products do not justify the capital investment of buying 500 units each. They sell one to five units per month. Let the supplier handle storage and fulfillment for these items while you focus your warehouse space and capital on high-performers.

3

Promote Winners to 3PL, Demote Losers to Dropship

Run this analysis monthly: which dropshipped products have hit consistent volume (30+ orders per month)? Those get promoted to 3PL fulfillment with bulk inventory. Which 3PL-warehoused products have slowed to a trickle? Consider running down the inventory and switching them back to dropshipping. This dynamic allocation of products between models ensures you are always optimizing for the best combination of margin, speed, and capital efficiency.

4

Use a Single Dashboard to Manage Both

The hybrid model only works if your order management system can route orders correctly — 3PL orders to the warehouse, dropship orders to the supplier — without manual intervention. Most modern e-commerce platforms (Shopify, WooCommerce) support this through fulfillment location settings and app integrations. Your 3PL handles one set of orders; your dropship supplier handles the rest. Tracking numbers from both sources push back to the platform and to the customer.

Making the Transition: Dropshipping to 3PL

If you are currently dropshipping and considering the move to 3PL fulfillment, here is the practical roadmap:

1

Identify Your Top 5-10 Products by Revenue

Pull your sales data from the last 90 days. Which products generate the most revenue and the most orders? These are the products you will transition first. Do not try to move your entire catalog at once. Start with your proven winners.

2

Source Directly from the Manufacturer

Your dropshipping supplier is a middleman. The manufacturer who actually produces your product sells it for 30-50% less. Contact the manufacturer directly (Alibaba, trade shows, industry directories) and negotiate bulk pricing. Your per-unit cost drop is where the margin improvement comes from.

3

Order Your First Bulk Shipment to a 3PL

Place your first production order and ship it directly to your 3PL warehouse. At Miami Alliance 3PL, there is no minimum order requirement — you can start with as little as 1-2 pallets. The 3PL receives your inventory, scans every unit into the WMS, and your products are live for fulfillment within days.

4

Route Orders for Those Products to the 3PL

Update your Shopify or WooCommerce fulfillment settings so that orders for your transitioned products route to the 3PL instead of the dropshipping supplier. Keep your remaining products on dropship fulfillment. You now have a hybrid model running automatically.

5

Measure, Optimize, and Expand

Track margins, shipping speeds, customer reviews, and return rates for your 3PL-fulfilled products versus your dropshipped products. The data will speak for itself. As you generate more profit from the 3PL products, reinvest that profit into transitioning additional products from dropship to 3PL. Within 6-12 months, your top 20-30 products should be fully 3PL-fulfilled while the long tail remains on dropship.

The transition is not an overnight flip — it is a gradual, data-driven migration. You do not need $50,000 on day one. You need enough capital to buy a first batch of your top 5 products and enough discipline to reinvest the higher margins back into expanding your 3PL-warehoused catalog. The best time to start the transition is when you have a product consistently selling 50+ units per month through dropshipping. At that point, the economics of 3PL fulfillment are unambiguously better.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is dropshipping or 3PL fulfillment more profitable?

3PL fulfillment typically produces higher profit margins per order because you buy inventory at wholesale prices and control your markup. Dropshipping has lower upfront costs but thinner margins — typically 15-30% versus 40-60% with owned inventory fulfilled by a 3PL. The breakeven point where 3PL becomes more profitable than dropshipping is usually around 100-200 orders per month, because the bulk shipping rates and branding advantages of 3PL fulfillment compound over time.

Can I use a 3PL for dropshipping?

Yes. A 3PL can function as your dropshipping fulfillment partner. You store your inventory at the 3PL warehouse, and when an order comes in through your Shopify, WooCommerce, or Amazon store, the 3PL picks, packs, and ships it directly to your customer under your brand name. This is sometimes called "private label dropshipping" or "branded dropshipping" because you own the inventory and control the entire customer experience, unlike traditional dropshipping where a third-party supplier ships generic products.

What is the difference between dropshipping and 3PL?

With dropshipping, you never touch the product — a supplier manufactures, stores, and ships it directly to your customer when an order is placed. With 3PL fulfillment, you purchase inventory upfront from a manufacturer, ship it to a third-party warehouse, and the 3PL stores and fulfills orders on your behalf. The key differences are inventory ownership (you own it with 3PL), profit margins (higher with 3PL), shipping speed (faster with 3PL since inventory is pre-positioned domestically), and branding (full control with 3PL, limited with dropshipping).

How much does 3PL fulfillment cost compared to dropshipping?

Dropshipping has near-zero startup costs — you pay only the product cost plus supplier shipping when an order is placed. 3PL fulfillment requires upfront inventory investment but has lower per-order costs: storage runs $15-$40 per pallet per month, pick and pack is $1.50-$5.00 per order, and shipping rates are 15-40% below retail through bulk carrier discounts. For a store doing 200 orders per month, total 3PL costs (excluding inventory purchase) run $500-$1,200 per month, but the higher margins on owned inventory usually make the total economics significantly better.

Can I combine dropshipping and 3PL in a hybrid model?

Absolutely. A hybrid model is one of the most effective strategies for growing e-commerce businesses. You keep your proven, high-volume products in a 3PL warehouse for fast, branded fulfillment with strong margins, while using dropshipping for new product launches, slow-moving SKUs, or test products where you do not want to commit to bulk inventory. Many Miami Alliance 3PL clients run this exact hybrid — their top 20 SKUs are warehoused for same-day fulfillment while their extended catalog of 50-100 SKUs is dropshipped from suppliers.
