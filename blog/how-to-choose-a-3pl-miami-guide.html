What Is a 3PL?

A 3PL (third-party logistics provider) is an outsourced partner that handles some or all of your supply chain operations — typically warehousing, inventory management, order fulfillment (pick, pack, and ship), and shipping. Instead of leasing your own warehouse, hiring your own staff, and managing your own logistics technology, you pay a 3PL to do it for you.

The model works because 3PLs achieve economies of scale that individual businesses can't. They negotiate volume shipping rates with carriers like UPS, FedEx, USPS, and DHL. They invest in warehouse management systems (WMS), barcode scanning infrastructure, and trained fulfillment teams. They spread the cost of warehouse space, insurance, security, and equipment across many clients — which means you pay a fraction of what it would cost to run these operations yourself.

For e-commerce brands, DTC companies, subscription box businesses, Amazon sellers, and wholesale distributors, a 3PL eliminates the biggest operational bottleneck in your business: getting products from your warehouse to your customer's doorstep, fast and accurately. If you want a deeper dive into 3PL fundamentals, read our guide on what a 3PL warehouse is and how it works.

But not every 3PL is the same. The provider you choose will directly impact your shipping times, order accuracy, customer satisfaction scores, and operating costs. That's why the selection process matters so much.

7 Key Factors When Choosing a Miami 3PL

After working with hundreds of brands and watching the Miami logistics market evolve, we've identified seven factors that separate great 3PL partnerships from disastrous ones. Evaluate every provider against these criteria before you sign anything.

1. Location & Proximity to Major Infrastructure

In logistics, location is everything. A Miami 3PL's physical address determines how fast your products move, how much you pay for freight, and which markets you can serve efficiently.

Here's what to look for in terms of proximity:

  • Port Miami: If you import goods by sea — especially from Latin America, the Caribbean, or Asia via the Panama Canal — you need a 3PL within a reasonable drayage distance of PortMiami. Container trucking costs increase dramatically beyond 30 miles. The industrial corridors in Medley and Doral are ideally positioned, typically 20-25 miles from port with direct highway access.
  • Miami International Airport (MIA): MIA is the largest international freight airport in the United States, handling over 2.5 million tons of cargo annually. If you receive air freight or need expedited international shipping, proximity to MIA is critical. Our facility at 8780 NW 100th ST, Medley, FL 33178 is just 8 miles from MIA — a 15-minute drive without traffic.
  • I-95 and the Florida Turnpike: Ground shipping to the rest of the U.S. depends on highway access. A 3PL near I-95 or the Turnpike means faster carrier pickups, more flexible cutoff times, and lower last-mile costs for regional delivery. From Medley, you have direct access to both major corridors.
  • Carrier hub proximity: UPS, FedEx, and USPS all have major distribution hubs in the Miami-Dade area. The closer your 3PL is to these hubs, the later the daily shipping cutoff can be — which means more orders ship same-day.

2. Warehouse Capacity & Scalability

Your 3PL needs to handle your current volume and your growth. This is one of the most overlooked factors in the selection process. Too many brands choose a 3PL that fits their needs today but can't scale when order volume doubles during a successful product launch or holiday season.

Key questions to ask about capacity:

  • Total square footage: How large is the facility? A 5,000 sq ft warehouse is fine for a micro-brand, but if you're growing, you'll outgrow it within a year. Look for 3PLs with 15,000+ sq ft that can allocate more space as your inventory grows.
  • Current utilization: A warehouse that's already 95% full has no room for your growth. Ask what percentage of their space is currently allocated and what their expansion plan looks like.
  • Flexible storage: Can they handle both palletized and shelved inventory? Do they offer bin locations for small items and floor storage for oversized products? A good 3PL optimizes storage based on your product dimensions and pick frequency.
  • Peak season capacity: What happens during Black Friday, Cyber Monday, or your seasonal peak? Do they hire temporary staff? Do they have overflow space? Or do they cap intake and leave you scrambling?

At Miami Alliance 3PL, our 20,000+ sq ft facility is designed for scalability. We maintain reserve capacity specifically for client growth and seasonal surges, so you never hit a ceiling when your business takes off.

3. Technology & Real-Time Inventory Tracking

In 2026, there is no excuse for a 3PL that doesn't offer real-time inventory visibility. If a provider can't show you exactly how many units of each SKU are in stock, where they are in the warehouse, and what orders are in the fulfillment pipeline — at any moment — they're operating with outdated infrastructure.

Here's what a modern 3PL technology stack should include:

  • Warehouse Management System (WMS): The backbone of any professional 3PL operation. A WMS manages receiving, putaway, picking routes, packing verification, and shipping label generation. Ask to see their WMS in action.
  • Customer portal/dashboard: You should be able to log in at any time and see real-time inventory counts, order statuses, shipment tracking numbers, and historical reports. If their "portal" is a shared Google Sheet, that's a hard no.
  • Barcode scanning: Every item should be scanned at receiving, during putaway, at picking, and at packing. This is what delivers 99%+ order accuracy. Manual processes without scanning are error-prone and outdated.
  • Automated alerts: Low stock notifications, order exceptions, and delivery confirmations should come to you automatically — not only when you call and ask.
  • Reporting and analytics: Monthly or on-demand reports covering inventory turns, order accuracy rates, shipping times, and cost per order. This data is essential for optimizing your supply chain.

Our inventory management system gives every client real-time visibility into their inventory, order fulfillment status, and shipping data through a dedicated customer portal. No guesswork, no waiting for email updates.

4. E-Commerce Integrations

If you sell online — and most brands using a 3PL do — your logistics provider needs to integrate directly with your sales channels. Manual order entry (copying orders from Shopify and emailing them to the warehouse) is a recipe for errors, delays, and lost sales.

The e-commerce integrations that matter most in 2026:

  • Shopify and Shopify Plus: Automatic order import, real-time inventory sync back to your store, and tracking number pushback to customers. This is table stakes for any 3PL serving DTC brands.
  • Amazon FBA Prep: If you sell on Amazon, your 3PL should be able to receive bulk inventory, label it to Amazon's strict FNSKU requirements, and ship it to Amazon fulfillment centers. Poor FBA prep leads to costly Amazon chargebacks. Learn more in our Amazon FBA prep guide.
  • WooCommerce: For brands running WordPress-based stores, a WooCommerce integration ensures orders flow directly to the warehouse without manual intervention.
  • Multi-channel support: If you sell on Shopify, Amazon, Walmart Marketplace, and your own website, your 3PL needs to pull orders from all channels into a single fulfillment queue. Otherwise, you're managing multiple workflows and risking inventory overselling.
  • API access: For brands with custom platforms or proprietary order management systems, API access allows custom integrations. Not every 3PL offers this, so ask if you need it.

Read our detailed guides on 3PL fulfillment for Shopify stores and 3PL for DTC brands for platform-specific advice.

5. Pricing Transparency

This is where many brands get burned. A 3PL quotes you an attractive per-order rate, and then your first invoice is 40% higher than expected because of fees that were never discussed upfront — setup fees, account management fees, minimum monthly charges, receiving surcharges, and "special handling" add-ons.

What transparent 3PL pricing looks like:

  • Storage fees: Clearly stated per-pallet or per-cubic-foot monthly rate. No ambiguity about how storage is measured.
  • Receiving fees: Per-pallet or per-unit rate for incoming inventory. Includes unloading, counting, inspection, and system entry.
  • Pick-and-pack fees: Per-order base rate plus per-additional-item rate. Should include standard packaging materials.
  • Shipping: Either pass-through carrier rates (you see the exact UPS/FedEx/USPS cost) or a clearly quoted handling fee per shipment.
  • No hidden fees: Setup fees, integration fees, account management fees, and minimum monthly charges should be disclosed before you sign. If they're not on the rate card, they don't exist.

6. Specialty Services

Basic 3PL services — receiving, storage, pick-pack-ship — are the minimum. What separates a good 3PL from a great one is the ability to handle the specialized needs that make your brand unique.

Specialty services to ask about:

  • Black wrapping / discreet packaging: Essential for luxury goods, adult products, high-value items, or any brand that prioritizes unboxing discretion. Not every 3PL offers this — those that do can add significant value for certain product categories. Learn more in our guide to black wrapping and discreet packaging.
  • Climate-controlled storage: If you sell supplements, cosmetics, food products, electronics, or anything temperature-sensitive, climate control isn't optional. Ask about temperature range, monitoring systems, and what happens during a power outage.
  • Kitting and assembly: Subscription boxes, product bundles, gift sets, and promotional kits all require assembly services. Your 3PL should be able to take individual components and build finished kits to your exact specifications. See our kitting and assembly services guide.
  • Custom packaging and inserts: Branded tissue paper, thank-you cards, promotional inserts, stickers — these details drive repeat purchases and customer loyalty. Your 3PL should handle them as part of the standard fulfillment workflow.
  • Returns processing: How does the 3PL handle returned items? Do they inspect, restock, quarantine, or dispose? A clear returns workflow prevents inventory discrepancies and speeds up customer refunds.
  • FBA prep and labeling: Amazon-bound shipments require precise labeling (FNSKU, suffocation warnings, polybagging) to avoid chargebacks. Your 3PL should know Amazon's requirements cold.

Check whether the 3PL charges extra for each specialty service or includes some of them in their standard rate. Some providers nickel-and-dime every add-on; others include kitting and custom packaging in the fulfillment fee.

7. Customer Service & Communication

This is the factor that every business owner says matters most — but it's the hardest to evaluate before you're actually a client. A 3PL with excellent warehouse operations but terrible communication will drive you crazy. You need a provider that's responsive, proactive, and treats your business like it matters.

What great 3PL customer service looks like:

  • Dedicated account manager: You should have one person who knows your account inside and out. Not a rotating support queue. Not a chatbot. A real human who picks up the phone.
  • Fast response times: Same-day response to emails, same-hour response to urgent issues. During business hours, your 3PL should be reachable by phone, email, or messaging platform within minutes.
  • Proactive communication: If a shipment is delayed, a SKU is running low, or there's an issue with an order, you should hear about it from your 3PL before you discover it yourself. Proactive ops updates are the hallmark of a well-run warehouse.
  • Onboarding support: The first 30 days with a new 3PL set the tone for the entire relationship. A good provider offers a structured onboarding process with dedicated support to get your inventory transferred, integrations connected, and first orders flowing smoothly.
  • Problem resolution: Mistakes happen in every warehouse. What matters is how the 3PL responds. Do they acknowledge errors immediately, fix them at their expense, and implement process changes to prevent recurrence? Or do they make excuses and leave you to deal with unhappy customers?

How to evaluate customer service before you sign: Ask for references. Talk to two or three current clients. Ask them specifically about response times, error handling, and whether they feel like a priority or an afterthought. You can also schedule a call with our team to experience firsthand how we communicate.

Red Flags to Watch For

You can learn as much about a 3PL from what they don't say as from what they do. Here are the warning signs that should make you walk away — no matter how good the rate card looks.

⚠️
Red Flag #1: No Technology Platform. If a 3PL can't show you a customer portal with real-time inventory and order tracking, they are not operating at a professional level. "We'll email you reports" or "you can call us anytime" are not substitutes for technology. In 2026, a real-time dashboard is the absolute minimum standard.
⚠️
Red Flag #2: Vague or Hidden Pricing. If a 3PL won't give you a detailed rate card until you sign a contract, or if they dodge questions about specific fees, expect invoice surprises. Transparent providers publish their pricing or provide instant quotes. If they won't put numbers on paper, there's a reason.
⚠️
Red Flag #3: Long-Term Contract Requirements. A 3PL that requires a 12-month or multi-year contract is betting that you'll be harder to retain once you're locked in. The best 3PLs operate on month-to-month agreements with 30-day notice periods — because they're confident enough in their service to earn your business every single month.
⚠️
Red Flag #4: No Scalability Plan. Ask: "What happens if my order volume triples in six months?" If the answer is vague or involves transferring you to a different facility, you'll face a painful transition right when your business is growing fastest. Your 3PL should have a clear plan for scaling your storage and fulfillment capacity without disrupting operations.
⚠️
Red Flag #5: Won't Let You Visit the Warehouse. This is a dealbreaker. Any legitimate 3PL welcomes facility tours. A refusal to let you see where your products will be stored and handled is a major red flag. You should see the actual warehouse floor, meet the team, and observe operations. If they make excuses, move on.

Other warning signs include: high staff turnover (ask how long the warehouse team has been there), inconsistent communication during the sales process (it won't get better after you sign), no references from current clients, and outdated technology that hasn't been updated in years.

Why Miami Is the Best Location for 3PL

If you're reading this guide, you're probably already considering Miami for your fulfillment operations. Here's why that's a smart decision — and why the Miami-Dade market has become one of the fastest-growing 3PL hubs in the country.

Gateway to Latin America and the Caribbean

Miami is the undisputed gateway for trade between the United States and Latin America. Over 70% of all U.S.-Latin America trade passes through South Florida. If your business serves markets in Brazil, Colombia, Mexico, Central America, or the Caribbean islands, having your 3PL in Miami gives you a massive logistical advantage. Shipping times to the Caribbean are 2-3 days by sea. Air freight to most Latin American capitals is overnight. No other U.S. city comes close to this connectivity. Read our full analysis of Miami-to-Latin America fulfillment strategies.

Port Miami — Florida's #1 Container Port

PortMiami handles over 1 million TEUs (twenty-foot equivalent units) of containerized cargo annually. It has direct shipping lanes to over 100 countries, deep-water berths that can accommodate the largest container vessels, and a post-Panamax tunnel that connects the port directly to the interstate highway system. For brands that import goods by ocean freight, proximity to PortMiami means faster container turns and lower drayage costs.

Miami International Airport — #1 International Freight Hub

MIA handles more international air cargo than any other airport in the United States. It's also the #1 airport in the country for freight from Latin America and the Caribbean. If you receive air freight shipments or need expedited international outbound shipping, being near MIA is a major advantage. The Medley/Doral industrial corridor sits just 8-12 miles from the airport, making same-day freight pickups and deliveries routine.

Foreign Trade Zones

Miami-Dade County is home to multiple Foreign Trade Zones (FTZs), including FTZ #281. Operating within or near an FTZ allows businesses to defer, reduce, or eliminate customs duties on imported goods — especially beneficial for brands that import raw materials or components and re-export finished products. If you're importing inventory from overseas, ask your 3PL whether they operate within an FTZ or can help you take advantage of one.

Domestic Shipping Reach

Miami provides 2-day ground shipping coverage to the entire Southeast U.S. — including the high-growth markets of Florida, Georgia, Texas, and the Carolinas. With major carrier hubs in the area, UPS Ground, FedEx Ground, and USPS Priority shipments from Miami reach most East Coast destinations within 2-3 business days. For brands whose customer base is concentrated in the Southeast or includes strong Latin American markets, Miami is the optimal fulfillment location.

Competitive Warehouse Rates

Compared to logistics hubs like Los Angeles, New Jersey, or Chicago, Miami — and especially the Medley/Doral industrial corridor — offers significantly lower warehouse lease rates while maintaining access to world-class port, airport, and highway infrastructure. This translates directly into lower storage costs for 3PL clients. You get top-tier logistics infrastructure at Sunbelt pricing.

Checklist: Questions to Ask Your 3PL Provider

Before you commit to any 3PL, use this checklist to evaluate them systematically. We recommend asking these questions during your initial call or warehouse tour and documenting the answers for side-by-side comparison if you're evaluating multiple providers.

Operations & Capabilities

  1. What is your total warehouse square footage, and what percentage is currently utilized?
  2. What is your documented order accuracy rate? (Look for 99%+ — ask for evidence, not claims.)
  3. What is your same-day shipping cutoff time? (Industry standard in Miami is 2:00 PM EST.)
  4. Which carriers do you work with, and what are your negotiated rate tiers?
  5. How do you handle peak season volume increases?
  6. Do you offer climate-controlled storage?
  7. Can you handle kitting, assembly, or custom packaging?

Technology & Integrations

  1. Can I see a live demo of your customer portal?
  2. Which e-commerce platforms do you integrate with natively? (Shopify, Amazon, WooCommerce, etc.)
  3. Do you offer real-time inventory tracking with low-stock alerts?
  4. Do you provide API access for custom integrations?
  5. How are tracking numbers communicated to my customers?

Pricing & Contracts

  1. Can you provide a complete rate card with all fees itemized?
  2. Are there setup fees, integration fees, or account management fees?
  3. Do you have minimum order requirements or monthly minimums?
  4. What is the contract length? (Month-to-month is ideal.)
  5. What is the notice period and process for termination?
  6. Are there peak-season surcharges?

Service & Relationships

  1. Will I have a dedicated account manager?
  2. What are your typical response times for support requests?
  3. How do you handle fulfillment errors? Who absorbs the cost?
  4. Can you provide references from 2-3 current clients?
  5. Can I tour the warehouse before signing?
  6. What does your onboarding process look like, and how long does it take?
  7. What happens to my inventory if I decide to leave?
✓
Pro Tip: Don't just check boxes — pay attention to how each 3PL answers these questions. A provider that responds with detailed, specific answers demonstrates operational maturity. One that gives vague responses or deflects is revealing something about how they'll communicate once you're a client.

Making Your Decision

Choosing a 3PL is one of the most consequential decisions a growing business makes. The right partner accelerates your growth, improves customer satisfaction, and frees you to focus on product development, marketing, and sales. The wrong partner creates a daily operational headache that bleeds into every part of your business.

Here's a simple framework for making your final decision:

  1. Narrow your list to 3-4 providers based on location, services, and technology capabilities.
  2. Request detailed rate cards from each and model your costs at current volume and at 2x projected volume.
  3. Visit each warehouse in person. Walk the floor, meet the team, and observe operations. You'll learn more in a 30-minute tour than in ten sales calls.
  4. Talk to references. Ask current clients about accuracy, communication, billing transparency, and how the 3PL handled a problem.
  5. Start small. If possible, send a test shipment of inventory and fulfill a few dozen orders before committing your full catalog. A trial run reveals operational reality.
