Q2 2026 Logistics Outlook: Hormuz Fallout, Carrier Surcharges, and Why Miami-Based Supply Chains Win

Miami Alliance 3PL Team March 26, 2026 9 min read

If you import, ship, or sell physical products in the United States, the next 90 days are about to test your supply chain like nothing before. Oil is trading above $126 a barrel. The Strait of Hormuz — the chokepoint for 20% of the world's petroleum — has been effectively blockaded since late February 2026. And this week, USPS announced something it has never done in its history: an 8% fuel surcharge on every parcel shipment, starting April 26.

For e-commerce sellers, importers, and brand operators, the question is no longer if shipping costs will rise, but how much and what can you do about it? This Q2 2026 logistics outlook breaks down the four major forces shaping your shipping budget this quarter — and explains why businesses with Miami-based supply chains are better positioned to weather the storm than those relying on traditional West Coast corridors.

The Hormuz Effect: How a Strait 21 Miles Wide Is Shaking Global Freight

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched coordinated strikes on Iranian military installations. Iran's response was swift: tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz dropped approximately 70% within days. Major container lines, including Hapag-Lloyd, suspended all transits through the strait. As of late March, the blockade remains in effect, and diplomatic channels have produced no clear timeline for resolution.

The Strait of Hormuz connects the Persian Gulf to the Gulf of Oman and the wider Indian Ocean. Before the crisis, roughly 21 million barrels of oil per day flowed through this narrow passage — about one-fifth of global petroleum consumption. The blockade has triggered cascading effects across every mode of freight transportation:

  • Ocean freight spot rates on affected routes have surged 40-60%, with container surcharges of $1,500-$4,000 per TEU on Asia-to-U.S. lanes
  • Transit times have increased by 10-14 days as vessels reroute around the Cape of Good Hope or through alternative corridors
  • Fuel prices hit $126/barrel (Brent crude), up from $78 in January — a 62% increase in under three months
  • Diesel prices in the U.S. have climbed past $5.40/gallon, directly increasing the cost of every trucking and last-mile delivery
  • Air freight capacity is tightening as shippers switch modes to avoid ocean delays, pushing air cargo rates up 18-25%

For businesses that source products from Asia, the Middle East, or Europe via Suez Canal routes, the cost impact is immediate and severe. But the effects don't stop at ocean freight — they ripple into every carrier surcharge, fuel adjustment, and accessorial fee in the domestic shipping network.

The Surcharge Pileup: USPS, UPS, FedEx, and What It Means for Your Margins

The Hormuz crisis didn't create carrier rate pressure — it poured gasoline on a fire that was already burning. Here's what the major carriers have announced for Q2 2026:

USPS: Historic 8% Fuel Surcharge (Effective April 26, 2026)

The U.S. Postal Service announced on March 25, 2026 that it will impose a temporary 8% fuel surcharge on all parcel shipments starting April 26. This is unprecedented — USPS has never applied a fuel surcharge to parcels before. The agency cited the inability to adjust base transportation rates fast enough to keep pace with surging diesel and jet fuel costs. For sellers shipping 5,000+ packages per month through USPS, this adds roughly $0.60-$1.20 per package depending on weight and zone.

UPS and FedEx: GRI + Demand Surcharges

Both UPS and FedEx implemented their annual General Rate Increase (GRI) of 5.9% in January 2026. On top of that, both carriers have layered additional demand surcharges and fuel adjustment factors that effectively bring the total year-over-year rate increase to 10-14% for most shippers. FedEx also quietly expanded its DIM weight divisor for ground shipments, increasing effective rates on lightweight, bulky items by an additional 3-7%.

Regional and Last-Mile Carriers

Regional carriers like OnTrac, LSO, and Spee-Dee are implementing their own fuel surcharges ranging from 5-12%. Even Amazon's internal shipping rates for FBA and SFP (Seller Fulfilled Prime) have seen accessorial fee increases of 8-15% in Q2 2026.

When you stack all of these increases, the average e-commerce seller should budget for a 12-20% increase in total shipping costs during Q2 2026 compared to Q4 2025. For a business shipping $50,000/month in freight, that's an additional $6,000-$10,000 per month bleeding from your margins.

The Section 122 Tariff Clock: 90 Days Until Expiration — or Extension

As if fuel surcharges weren't enough, importers are simultaneously navigating the 10% temporary global tariff imposed under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974. This tariff went into effect on February 24, 2026 and is set to expire on July 24, 2026 — right in the middle of Q3 planning season.

The tariff was the Administration's pivot after the Supreme Court struck down the IEEPA-based tariffs on February 20. While the 10% rate is lower than the previous IEEPA rates (which ranged from 20-60% on Chinese goods), it applies globally — meaning goods from allies like the EU, UK, Japan, and Canada are now subject to duties they didn't face before.

For logistics planning in Q2 2026, the tariff creates a strategic dilemma:

  • Pre-load inventory before July 24? If the tariff expires, you've tied up capital in excess stock. If it's extended, you saved 10% on landed costs.
  • Use bonded warehousing? Goods stored in a customs bonded warehouse defer duty payment until withdrawal, giving you flexibility to time your imports around tariff changes.
  • Pursue IEEPA refunds? If you paid IEEPA duties between February 2025 and February 2026, the Court of International Trade has ordered refunds. CBP is building the CAPE portal (expected mid-April) to process claims. Our IEEPA refund guide walks through the process.

The uncertainty alone is a cost. Businesses that can't model their landed costs with confidence end up over-ordering, under-ordering, or pricing their products incorrectly — all of which erode margins in an already tight environment.

Why Miami-Based Supply Chains Have a Structural Advantage Right Now

Not all shipping lanes are equally affected by the Hormuz blockade. And not all warehouse locations offer the same insulation against rising domestic carrier costs. Miami — specifically the Medley/Doral corridor near PortMiami and Miami International Airport — gives businesses several compounding advantages in Q2 2026:

1. Atlantic Routes Bypass Hormuz Entirely

PortMiami's primary trade lanes run to Latin America, the Caribbean, Europe, and the U.S. East Coast. None of these routes transit the Strait of Hormuz. While West Coast ports like Long Beach and Oakland are seeing severe congestion and surcharges on Asia-Pacific routes, Miami's Atlantic-facing corridors remain operational with minimal disruption. Importers sourcing from Colombia, Brazil, Central America, or Europe face far less freight volatility from a Miami base.

2. Nearshoring Is Accelerating — and Miami Is the Hub

The combination of tariff chaos and the Hormuz crisis is pushing more brands to source from Latin American manufacturing centers — Mexico, Colombia, the Dominican Republic, and Guatemala. Miami is the natural routing, warehousing, and distribution hub for nearshored goods entering the U.S. market. If your supply chain is shifting toward LATAM sourcing, positioning inventory in Miami cuts both transit time and shipping cost.

3. Zone Optimization Reduces Last-Mile Costs

Miami provides 2-day ground shipping coverage to approximately 80% of the U.S. population. For e-commerce brands, this means lower effective shipping zones — and with every carrier implementing fuel surcharges, zone distance is the biggest variable you can control. Shipping a 2-lb package from Miami to Atlanta (Zone 3) costs significantly less than shipping the same package from Los Angeles to Atlanta (Zone 7). At the USPS's new 8% surcharge, that zone difference translates to real savings on every order.

4. PortMiami Infrastructure Is Expanding

PortMiami is in the middle of a $400 million+ infrastructure expansion, with new deep-water berths, expanded container yards, and faster customs processing. While West Coast ports struggle with congestion, PortMiami is actively increasing throughput capacity — a structural advantage that will matter even more as ocean routes continue to shift eastward.

5. Florida's Tax and Business Environment

No state income tax. Business-friendly regulatory environment. A bilingual workforce experienced in international trade. These aren't new advantages, but they become more relevant when every dollar of operating cost matters — which is exactly the environment Q2 2026 presents.

7 Strategies to Protect Your Margins in Q2 2026

Here's what proactive businesses are doing right now to insulate their operations from the Q2 cost surge:

  1. Audit your carrier mix immediately. If you're shipping 80%+ through a single carrier, you're overexposed to that carrier's surcharge structure. A 3PL partner with multi-carrier rate shopping can automatically route each package to the cheapest qualified option — saving 12-18% on average.
  2. Reposition inventory closer to customers. Every zone you eliminate from your average shipment reduces cost. If you're fulfilling from a single West Coast warehouse, consider adding an East Coast or Southeast node. Miami gives you coverage for Florida, the Southeast, the Mid-Atlantic, and Latin America from one location.
  3. Negotiate fuel surcharge caps. If you have a direct carrier contract, push for a fuel surcharge cap or ceiling. Many carriers will negotiate this for committed volume. Your 3PL may already have negotiated caps as part of their enterprise agreements.
  4. Optimize packaging to reduce DIM weight. With FedEx expanding DIM weight divisors, right-sizing your packaging is no longer optional. Every cubic inch of wasted space costs money. Invest in custom box sizes or poly mailers for lightweight items.
  5. Consolidate shipments where possible. For B2B or wholesale channels, consolidating multiple orders into fewer, larger shipments reduces per-unit freight costs. Pick and pack services with intelligent batch processing make this operationally feasible.
  6. Pre-position safety stock before surcharge effective dates. If you know USPS surcharges hit April 26, ensure your fast-moving SKUs are already in warehouse and ready to ship. This is especially critical for Amazon FBA sellers who need to maintain Prime eligibility.
  7. Explore bonded warehousing for tariff flexibility. If you're importing goods subject to the Section 122 tariff, a customs bonded warehouse lets you defer duty payment until goods are withdrawn for sale — giving you optionality if the tariff expires on July 24.
