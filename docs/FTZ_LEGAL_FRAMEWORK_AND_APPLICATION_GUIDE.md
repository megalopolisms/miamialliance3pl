## 1. Federal Legal Framework

### 1.1 Foreign-Trade Zones Act of 1934 (19 U.S.C. 81a-81u)

The FTZ Act, codified at 19 U.S.C. Sections 81a through 81u, establishes the legal authority for foreign-trade zones in the United States. It was originally enacted on June 18, 1934.

**Key Sections:**

| Section | Title                               | Description                                                                                                                       |
| ------- | ----------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 81a     | Definitions                         | Defines "foreign-trade zone," "zone," "Board," "grantee," and related terms                                                       |
| 81b     | Establishment of Zones              | Authorizes the Board "to grant to corporations the privilege of establishing, operating, and maintaining foreign-trade zones"     |
| 81c     | Exemption from Customs Laws         | Merchandise brought into a zone is exempt from customs laws when subsequently exported, destroyed, or sent into customs territory |
| 81d     | Customs Officers and Guards         | Provides for assignment of customs officers to supervise zones                                                                    |
| 81e     | Vessels Entering or Leaving Zones   | Governs vessel movements in and out of zones                                                                                      |
| 81f     | Applications for Zone Establishment | Prescribes how applications shall be made to the Board                                                                            |
| 81g     | Granting of Applications            | Sets criteria for Board approval of applications                                                                                  |
| 81h     | Rules and Regulations               | Authorizes the Board to make rules and regulations                                                                                |
| 81i-81k | Cooperation with Other Agencies     | Governs inter-agency coordination                                                                                                 |
| 81l     | Agreements on Property Use          | Governs property agreements for zone operation                                                                                    |
| 81m     | Facilities                          | Authorizes construction and maintenance of zone facilities                                                                        |
| 81n     | Permission for Others to Use Zones  | Allows operators to permit third parties to use zone facilities                                                                   |
| 81o     | Zone Operation as Public Utility    | Zones operate on a "public utility basis, with published rates"                                                                   |
| 81p     | Zone Residents                      | Provisions regarding zone residents                                                                                               |
| 81q     | Accounts and Recordkeeping          | Annual reporting requirements to the Board and Congress                                                                           |
| 81r     | Transfer of Grants                  | Governs transfer of zone grants to other entities                                                                                 |
| 81s     | Revocation of Grants                | Conditions under which the Board may revoke a zone grant                                                                          |
| 81t     | Offenses                            | Penalties for violations of zone laws                                                                                             |
| 81u     | Separability                        | Standard severability clause                                                                                                      |

**FTZ Board Composition:**

- Secretary of Commerce (Chairman and Executive Officer)
- Secretary of the Treasury
- Secretary of the Army

### 1.2 Trade and Tariff Act of 1984 Amendments

The Trade and Tariff Act of 1984 (P.L. 98-573, signed October 30, 1984) amended the FTZ Act to:

1. **Tax Exemption:** Exempted from state and local ad valorem taxation: (a) tangible personal property imported from outside the U.S. and held in a foreign-trade zone, and (b) tangible personal property produced in the U.S. and held in a zone for exportation
2. **Bicycle Component Restriction:** Provided that bicycle component parts shall not be exempt under the FTZ customs exemption unless re-exported
3. **Production Equipment Deferral:** (Added in 1996 amendment) Allowed deferral and possible reduction of duties on production equipment imported into an FTZ

### 1.3 Alternative Site Framework (ASF) -- 2012 Final Rule

The ASF was adopted through a final rule published February 28, 2012, in the Federal Register (77 FR 12139). This fundamentally restructured how FTZ sites are designated.

**Key ASF Features:**

- Replaced the traditional site-specific zone designation with a more flexible "service area" approach
- Zones reorganize under ASF to define a geographic service area (typically county-wide)
- Within the service area, sites are designated as either "Magnet Sites" (general-purpose) or "Usage-Driven Sites" (company-specific)
- Usage-driven sites can be designated through "Minor Boundary Modifications" (MBMs) -- a streamlined process
- MBM applications are processed within 30 days of receipt of a complete application plus CBP concurrence
- Each ASF zone has a 2,000-acre activation limit

### 2.1 Complete Structure

| Subpart | Title                                                   | Sections        | Description                                                         |
| ------- | ------------------------------------------------------- | --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **A**   | Scope, Definitions and Authority                        | 400.1 - 400.7   | Foundational definitions, Board authority, Executive Secretary role |
| **B**   | Ability to Establish Zone; Limitations and Restrictions | 400.11 - 400.16 | Who can apply, number/location of zones, activation limits          |
| **C**   | Applications to Establish and Modify Authority          | 400.21 - 400.29 | Application requirements, content, types, fees                      |
| **D**   | Procedures for Application Evaluation and Reviews       | 400.31 - 400.38 | Evaluation procedures, comment periods, examiner reports            |
| **E**   | Operation of Zones and Administrative Requirements      | 400.41 - 400.49 | Zone operations, CBP coordination, uniform treatment                |
| **F**   | Records, Reports, Notice, Hearings and Information      | 400.51 - 400.54 | Annual reports, Federal Register notices, hearings, records         |
| **G**   | Penalties and Appeals to the Board                      | 400.61 - 400.63 | Penalty procedures, appeal rights                                   |

### 2.2 Key Sections for Applications

**Section 400.21 -- Application to Establish a Zone:**

- Application letter required, dated within 6 months of submission, signed by authorized officer
- Must describe: relationship to state enabling legislation, specific authority requested, proposed sites, project background, international trade connections
- Required detailed contents:
  - **(a) Legal Authority:** State enabling legislation, applicant's charter, certified governing body resolution (dated within 6 months)
  - **(b) Site Descriptions:** Size, location, address, existing/proposed structures, construction timelines, proximity to CBP ports of entry, zoning, environmental considerations, expansion possibilities
  - **(c) Operations and Financing:** Site ownership documentation, operational plans, commitment to CBP automated systems, financing summary
  - **(d) Economic Justification:** Community trade goals, economic profile, anticipated zone impacts, separate justification per site, evidence of user interest, letters of intent from prospective firms
  - **(e) Maps and Site Plans:** State/county maps, detailed site plans with zone boundaries marked in red, relationship maps for existing zone modifications
- Draft applications may be submitted to Executive Secretary for preliminary review
- Final submissions via email or prescribed method with all attachments

**Section 400.24 -- Application for Expansion or Other Modification:**

- **(a) General:** Grantees may apply for zone expansion or modification, including under ASF procedures
- **(b) Major Modifications:** Must follow full application requirements of Section 400.21
- **(c) Minor Modifications:** Submitted in letter form with documentation as determined by Executive Secretary; includes minor boundary revisions, subzone designations; processed within 30 days
- **(d) Other Revisions:** Activation limit changes, restriction modifications, voluntary termination -- submitted in letter form

**Section 400.27 -- Criteria for Evaluating Applications for Production Authority:**

- Market conditions and price sensitivity
- Degree and nature of foreign competition
- Intra-industry and intra-firm trade
- Effect on exports and imports
- Ability to conduct proposed activity outside U.S.
- Net effect on U.S. employment and economy
- Technology transfers and investment effects
- Significant public benefit(s) analysis

**Section 400.29 -- Application Fees:**

| Application Type                                   | Fee Amount |
| -------------------------------------------------- | ---------- |
| Additional general-purpose zones                   | $3,200     |
| Subzones (non-production or fewer than 3 products) | $4,000     |
| Subzones (production with 3+ products)             | $6,500     |
| Expansions (Section 400.24(b))                     | $1,600     |

Fees must be paid before docketing; method specified by Executive Secretary.

## 3. CBP Regulations (19 CFR Part 146)

### 3.1 Complete Structure

| Subpart | Title                                      | Key Content                                                      |
| ------- | ------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **A**   | General Provisions                         | Operator responsibility, zone changes, CBP oversight             |
| **B**   | Inventory Control and Recordkeeping System | System requirements, procedures manual, record retention         |
| **C**   | Admission of Merchandise to a Zone         | CBP Form 214, examination, permit procedures                     |
| **D**   | Status of Merchandise                      | Zone-restricted status, privileged/non-privileged foreign status |
| **E**   | Handling of Merchandise in a Zone          | Manipulation, manufacture, exhibition, destruction               |
| **F**   | Transfer of Merchandise from a Zone        | Entry procedures, weekly entry, direct delivery                  |
| **G**   | Penalties                                  | Penalty provisions for violations                                |
| **H**   | Petroleum Refineries                       | Special provisions for refineries                                |

### 3.2 Key Operator Requirements (19 CFR 146.4)

The zone operator is responsible for:

- Supervision of all zone activities
- Compliance with all FTZ regulations
- Maintaining inventory control and recordkeeping systems
- Submitting procedures manual to Port Director
- Executing Operator's Bond (CBP Form 301)

### 3.3 Inventory Control and Recordkeeping System (19 CFR 146, Subpart B)

The system must be capable of:

1. Accounting for ALL merchandise: admitted, stored, manipulated, manufactured, destroyed, transferred, and removed
2. Producing accurate and timely reports and documents
3. Identifying shortages and overages with sufficient detail (quantity, description, tariff classification, zone status, value)
4. Providing information necessary for entry of merchandise into Customs territory
5. Providing audit trail from admission through disposition

**Procedures Manual Requirements:**

- English language copy to Port Director
- Must be kept current; changes submitted at implementation
- Must cover: receiving, storing, processing, removing merchandise
- Must describe inventory system meeting CBP requirements
- Must describe methods for tracking merchandise to protect U.S. revenue

**Record Retention:**

- All records must be retained for 5 years after merchandise removal from zone
- Records must be readily available for CBP review at the zone

**Annual Requirements:**

- Physical inventory of all merchandise in the zone (at least annually)
- Prior notification of inventory dates to Customs
- Annual internal review of the inventory control and recordkeeping system
- Report to Port Director of any deficiency and corrective action

## 5. Pro Se Application Strategy

### 5.1 Critical Insight: You Do NOT File Directly with the FTZ Board

**The most important thing to understand:** Unlike court filings, FTZ applications are NOT filed pro se by the applicant directly. The application to the FTZ Board must be submitted by the **grantee** (FTZ 281 = Miami-Dade County/PortMiami) on behalf of the applicant (Miami Alliance 3PL).

**What "pro se" means in this context:** You prepare your own application materials, negotiate your own grantee/operator agreement, and manage the CBP activation process WITHOUT hiring an FTZ consultant or attorney. You are the principal drafting and coordinating all materials, but the grantee formally submits to the Board.

### 5.2 Three-Phase Process for FTZ 281

#### Phase I: Application with PortMiami (Local Grantee)

**What you do:**

1. Contact PortMiami FTZ staff at 305-347-4947 or Sonless.Martin@miamidade.gov
2. Request a pre-application meeting to discuss your plans
3. Complete the FTZ 281 Application form
4. Pay the $2,500 application fee
5. Provide required documentation:
   - Company information and corporate documents
   - Site address (8780 NW 100th ST, Medley, FL 33178)
   - Proposed square footage/acreage
   - Summary of planned FTZ activities (warehousing, distribution, inventory management)
   - Proof of right to use the site (lease or deed)
   - Detailed site map with boundaries marked in red
   - Current zoning documentation
   - Statement that FTZ status is not a precondition for site activity

**Timeline:** Varies; establish early contact for fastest processing.

#### Phase II: FTZ Board Approval (Federal)

**What happens:**

1. PortMiami (grantee) files the ASF Minor Boundary Modification with the FTZ Board
2. The Executive Secretary reviews the application for completeness
3. If complete, the application is docketed
4. CBP is consulted for concurrence
5. Federal Register notice is published inviting public comment
6. Decision issued within 30 days of complete application receipt + CBP comments

**What you prepare (grantee submits on your behalf):**

- Completed ASF MBM application (Word format from trade.gov)
- All required attachments and maps
- Economic justification for the site
- Evidence of user interest (your own business plan and import data)

**FTZ Board Fee:** $0 for usage-driven site MBM (fees apply primarily to subzone and production authority applications)

**Timeline:** ~30 days for MBM after complete submission.

#### Phase III: CBP Activation

**What you do:**

1. Submit activation request to the CBP Port Director at Port of Miami
2. Provide:
   - Signed company letter requesting activation
   - Supporting letter from PortMiami (grantee)
   - Completed CBP background investigation forms for company officers and key FTZ personnel
   - Facility blueprint
   - FTZ Procedures Manual (inventory control and recordkeeping system)
   - Executed FTZ Operator's Bond (CBP Form 301, minimum $50,000, often $100,000)
3. CBP Port Director orders suitability survey (physical security inspection)
4. Upon approval of activation and acceptance of bond, the site is activated

**Timeline:** Varies (typically 2-6 months for CBP activation, depending on local CBP workload).

### 5.3 Key Sections Requiring Legal Precision

1. **Grantee/Operator Agreement:** The contract between you and PortMiami -- carefully review all terms; this governs your obligations
2. **FTZ Procedures Manual:** Must meet all CBP requirements under 19 CFR Part 146, Subpart B; this is the most technically demanding document
3. **Bond Execution:** CBP Form 301 conditions under 19 CFR 113.73; requires surety company
4. **Economic Justification:** Must demonstrate public benefit and need for zone services

### 5.4 Common Rejection/Delay Reasons

Based on GAO-19-91 findings and FTZ Board practice:

1. **Incomplete Applications:** Missing required attachments, maps not properly marked
2. **Grantee Not Included:** FTZ Board will not review if grantee is not copied
3. **Inadequate Economic Justification:** Failing to demonstrate public benefit
4. **CBP Concurrence Issues:** Security or suitability concerns with the facility
5. **Incorrect Application Type:** Filing a full application when an MBM is appropriate, or vice versa
6. **Missing Right-to-Use Documentation:** No proof of lease/ownership
7. **Inconsistent Information:** Discrepancies between application and supporting documents
8. **Procedures Manual Deficiencies:** Inadequate inventory control systems for CBP requirements
9. **Bond Issues:** Insufficient bond amount or unacceptable surety

### 5.5 Economic Justification Structure

For a 3PL warehouse/distribution operation, the economic justification should address:

1. **Community Trade Impact:**
   - Miami's role as a major international trade hub
   - Volume of imports/exports flowing through the region
   - Your facility's contribution to the local trade ecosystem

2. **Employment Impact:**
   - Current and projected employment at the facility
   - Types of jobs (warehouse workers, logistics coordinators, etc.)
   - Wage levels and benefits

3. **User Interest and Demand:**
   - Clients who will utilize FTZ procedures (e.g., Pablo Nicotine Pouches, other importers)
   - Letters of intent from prospective FTZ users
   - Projected import volumes and duty savings

4. **Public Benefit:**
   - Duty deferral allowing competitive pricing
   - Inventory management efficiencies
   - Re-export facilitation
   - Contribution to local economy through reduced landed costs for importers

5. **Site Suitability:**
   - Proximity to Port of Miami and Miami International Airport
   - Existing warehouse infrastructure
   - Transportation access (roads, highways)
   - Security infrastructure

### 5.6 Environmental Review (NEPA)

**FTZ designations generally do NOT trigger a full NEPA environmental review.** The FTZ Board is not a "major federal action significantly affecting the quality of the human environment" for warehousing/distribution operations. However:

- The application must address "environmental considerations" as part of the site description (15 CFR 400.21(d)(2))
- This typically means noting existing environmental permits, zoning compliance, and absence of environmental hazards
- If production activity is requested later, additional environmental analysis may be required

### 5.7 Federal Register Notice

**You do not handle this.** The FTZ Board's Executive Secretary publishes the Federal Register notice. For ASF MBM applications:

- A notice is published in the Federal Register inviting public comment
- Comment periods typically run 30-60 days
- No objections typically received for routine warehouse/distribution operations
- The grantee and Executive Secretary handle publication logistics

## 7. CBP Requirements for Activation

### 7.1 CBP Forms

| Form               | Title                                                                  | Purpose                                                                                                     |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **CBP Form 214**   | Application for Foreign-Trade Zone Admission and/or Status Designation | Used to admit merchandise into the zone; uniquely and sequentially numbered                                 |
| **CBP Form 214-A** | Statistical Copy                                                       | Transmitted to Bureau of Census                                                                             |
| **CBP Form 216**   | Application for Foreign-Trade Zone Activity Permit                     | Used to request annual blanket permit for FTZ activity (manipulation, manufacture, exhibition, destruction) |
| **CBP Form 301**   | Customs Bond                                                           | FTZ Operator's Bond with conditions under 19 CFR 113.73                                                     |
| **CF 3171**        | Application to Unlade                                                  | Required when merchandise is unladen in zone directly from importing carrier                                |

### 7.2 Activation Checklist

- [ ] FTZ Board approval of site designation (Phase II complete)
- [ ] Grantee/Operator Agreement signed with PortMiami
- [ ] Written activation request to CBP Port Director (Port of Miami)
- [ ] Supporting letter from PortMiami (grantee)
- [ ] Background investigation forms for company officers and key FTZ personnel
- [ ] Facility blueprint showing zone boundaries
- [ ] FTZ Procedures Manual (inventory control and recordkeeping system)
- [ ] Surety bond obtained (FTZ Operator's Bond, CBP Form 301)
- [ ] CBP suitability survey completed and passed
- [ ] Port Director approval received
- [ ] Bond executed and accepted by Port Director
- [ ] Site declared "activated" -- merchandise may now be admitted in zone status

### 7.3 FTZ Operator's Bond

**Type:** Continuous bond on CBP Form 301 with conditions of 19 CFR 113.73
**Minimum Amount:** $50,000
**Typical Amount:** $100,000 for new FTZ operators (set by Port Director)
**Surety:** Must be underwritten by a Treasury-listed surety company
**Cost:** Annual premium typically 1-2% of bond amount ($500-$2,000/year for a $100,000 bond)
**Discount:** NAFTZ members may access discounted bond rates through the National Association of Foreign Trade-Zones

### 7.4 Physical Security Requirements

CBP's "Physical Security Guidelines for CBP Bonded Facilities" apply. These replaced the older T.D. 72-56 standards. The guidelines are described as "minimum guidelines necessary in order to assure the highest degree of security of the cargo being stored at these facilities."

**General Requirements:**

- Perimeter fencing or equivalent physical barriers
- CCTV surveillance system
- Alarm/intrusion detection system
- Controlled access points
- Adequate lighting
- Secure storage areas for high-value merchandise
- Visitor management procedures

**CBP Suitability Survey:**

- Ordered by Port Director as condition of activation approval
- Conducted by CBP officer (Cargo Security Survey)
- Assesses security, suitability, and fitness of facility
- Evaluates both physical and procedural security standards
- Results in pass/fail determination; deficiencies must be corrected before activation

### 7.5 FTZ Procedures Manual Requirements

The manual must cover:

1. **Organizational Structure:** Company organization chart, FTZ personnel roles and responsibilities
2. **Admission Procedures:** How merchandise is received, inspected, and admitted on CBP Form 214
3. **Inventory Control:** System description (manual or automated), lot tracking, status tracking
4. **Manipulation/Manufacturing:** Procedures for any manipulation or manufacturing activity (if applicable)
5. **Removal Procedures:** How merchandise is transferred out of the zone (entry, export, destruction)
6. **Recordkeeping:** What records are maintained, where, how long (minimum 5 years)
7. **Physical Security:** Description of security measures, access controls, surveillance
8. **Annual Physical Inventory:** Procedures for conducting annual inventory with CBP notification
9. **Internal Review:** Annual self-audit procedures and reporting to Port Director
10. **Emergency Procedures:** Handling of damaged, stolen, or missing merchandise

### 8.6 GAO Findings on FTZ Board (GAO-19-91)

In 2019, the GAO found that the FTZ Board's examiner reports failed to document consideration of all required criteria under Section 400.27. The Board updated its procedures in November 2018 in response. This means:

- **Current applications face more rigorous documented evaluation**
- **All criteria in Section 400.27 must be addressed in your application**
- **The Board is now more transparent about evaluation factors**

  - Letters of intent from prospective FTZ users (clients who import)

- [ ] Attend pre-application meeting with PortMiami FTZ staff
- [ ] Complete FTZ 281 Application form
- [ ] Prepare site map with zone boundaries marked in red
- [ ] Compile all required attachments
- [ ] Submit application and $2,500 fee to PortMiami
- [ ] Execute Grantee/Operator Agreement with PortMiami
- [ ] Obtain support letters from local jurisdictions and organizations

### Phase II: FTZ Board Designation (Weeks 7-12)

- [ ] PortMiami prepares and submits ASF MBM application to FTZ Board
- [ ] FTZ Board reviews for completeness
- [ ] Federal Register notice published
- [ ] CBP concurrence obtained
- [ ] Executive Secretary issues designation decision (target: 30 days from complete submission)
- [ ] Receive official Usage-Driven Site designation

### Phase III: CBP Activation (Weeks 13-26)

- [ ] Prepare FTZ Procedures Manual
- [ ] Obtain FTZ Operator's Bond (CBP Form 301, surety bond)
- [ ] Complete CBP background investigation forms for all relevant personnel
- [ ] Submit activation request package to CBP Port Director (Port of Miami)
- [ ] CBP conducts suitability survey (physical security inspection)
- [ ] Address any deficiencies identified in survey
- [ ] Port Director approves activation and accepts bond
- [ ] **SITE IS ACTIVATED -- Begin FTZ operations**

### Phase IV: Operational Setup (Ongoing)

- [ ] Train staff on FTZ procedures
- [ ] Implement inventory control system
- [ ] File initial CBP Form 214 admissions for merchandise
- [ ] File annual CBP Form 216 for blanket activity permit
- [ ] Conduct first physical inventory
- [ ] Submit first annual report to FTZ Board
- [ ] Schedule annual internal review of ICRS
