The Factory Logistics Problem

Manufacturing and distribution are fundamentally different operations — trying to run both under one roof creates bottlenecks in both

Factories Are Built to Manufacture, Not to Ship

Every hour your production team spends on logistics — scheduling pickups, printing labels, managing returns, dealing with retailer compliance — is an hour not spent on what actually drives your revenue: manufacturing. The more channels you sell through, the worse the logistics drag becomes. A single warehouse operation that sits between your production line and your customers eliminates the bottleneck entirely.

  • ⚠️ Warehouse space constraints — Production floor space gets consumed by finished goods waiting to ship, reducing your manufacturing capacity and creating safety hazards
  • ⚠️ Shipping complexity — Managing carrier relationships, rate shopping, label generation, and tracking across multiple channels requires dedicated logistics staff you shouldn't need to hire
  • ⚠️ Retailer compliance — Walmart, Target, Amazon Vendor, and Costco each have unique routing guides, labeling standards, and EDI requirements that change regularly
  • ⚠️ Returns handling — Customer returns arriving at your factory disrupt production workflows and create inventory confusion between new and returned goods
  • ⚠️ Multi-channel distribution — Selling through retail, wholesale, Amazon, your own website, and marketplaces means different fulfillment logic for every channel
  • ⚠️ Inventory visibility — Without a centralized system, you lose track of what's in production, what's in storage, what's allocated, and what's available to promise across channels

Factory 3PL — Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions from manufacturers evaluating third-party logistics for their distribution

How does a factory 3PL work?

You ship your production output — whether by truckload, LTL, or container — directly to our Miami warehouse. We receive, inspect, and store your inventory. From there, we fulfill orders to all your channels: retail stores, wholesale distributors, e-commerce customers, and marketplace buyers. You focus on manufacturing while we handle every step from warehouse dock to customer doorstep.

Can you handle truckload receiving?

Yes. We handle full truckload (TL) and less-than-truckload (LTL) receiving with appointment scheduling. Our dock can accommodate 53-foot trailers and ocean containers. Every inbound shipment goes through SKU verification, quantity confirmation, and condition inspection before putaway into storage.

Do you support retailer routing guides?

Yes. We ship to major retailers including Walmart, Target, Costco, Amazon Vendor Central, and regional chains following their specific routing guides, labeling requirements, and EDI specifications. Routing guide compliance is built into our standard process — not a premium add-on.

What about quality inspection?

We can inspect incoming goods at receiving and isolate defective or damaged units before they enter distribution. Inspection protocols are customized per manufacturer — from visual inspection and count verification to condition grading. Defective units are quarantined and documented with photo evidence for your records.

How do you handle seasonal demand spikes?

Flexible warehouse space and staffing scale with your production cycles and peak seasons. You don't need to lease additional warehouse space or hire temporary labor for Q4 surges, promotional launches, or new product rollouts. Our infrastructure absorbs the spike, and you scale back down without penalty when demand normalizes.

Can you support both domestic and international distribution?

Yes. We ship domestically to all 50 states via major carriers (UPS, FedEx, USPS, freight) and handle international distribution through our Miami hub. Miami's proximity to Port Miami and Miami International Airport makes it an ideal gateway for domestic ground shipping and international air/ocean freight to Latin America, the Caribbean, and beyond.
